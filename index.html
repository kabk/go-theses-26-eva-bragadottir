Introduction

“I ain’t reading all that,” I mutter, chuckling at my own meme reference as I swipe past a TikTok video drowning in text. Approximately three seconds into the next video on my feed, what I just did hits me. I encountered something that would’ve taken maybe ten seconds to engage with, and reflexively scrolled past it, already hunting for something more digestible. Something that would satisfy my appetite for information that I can take in fast and effortlessly.
It occurs to me that I’ve been using this method of absent-minded scrolling to reach a state of fulfilment, but without success. I don’t usually quit scrolling because I feel satisfied with the input it has provided me with, but rather because of some vague sense of “enough” kicking in. The realization sits heavy: I’ve trained myself to reject anything requiring more effort than a glance, and now my mind is starving on a diet of empty information.
Since then, I’ve looked at the body and the mind as parallel digestive systems. Fundamental systems shaped by the same values: efficiency, speed, discipline, and control. This project focuses on the space where nourishment becomes confused with optimization, and explores a culture that teaches us how to consume, but not how to be fed. Ultimately, it asks how graphic design can create friction that exposes the gap between consuming information and being nourished by it, within a culture that prioritises speed, optimization, and constant intake.

Background

I read 1984 when I was fourteen, and George Orwell’s dystopian vision of a future where individual freedom is subjugated to societal conformity has stayed with me. I've since been wary of parallels between modern society and the world described in the literary classic. Because of this, my attention was immediately caught when I encountered the following passage:
“Orwell warns that we will be overcome by an externally imposed oppression. But in Huxley's vision, no Big Brother is required to deprive people of their autonomy, maturity and history. As he saw it, people will come to love their oppression, to adore the technologies that undo their capacities to think.”
The passage is from Amusing Ourselves to Death, a 1985 book by media theorist Neil Postman. Postman is referring to Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World, a dystopian future comparable to that of 1984, but achieved through pleasure and distraction instead of oppression. In Amusing Ourselves to Death, Postman warns about the cultural consequences of communication systems that prioritize entertainment over reflection. He describes “present-centered mediums” which deliver fragmented pieces of information without context or continuity, focusing on present events. He argues that this fragmentation and lack of context makes it difficult for one to fully take information in. Communication moves quickly from one fragment to the next, leaving little time to question or evaluate what is being presented. In this sense, information is taken in rapidly; consumed rather than digested.

Chapter 1: Breaking into bites

Since realizing how disconnected I felt with my own consumption of information, I’ve found myself in the habit of breaking information down; fragmenting it manually – not to simplify it, but to deepen my understanding of it. I compare it with breaking food down; isolating nutrients for absorption and discarding the unnecessary waste. This practice began with listening to audiobooks while drawing and writing intuitively. I would often pause and rewind the book to write down snippets I was struck by, and react to them with my own thoughts or drawings.
This practice began when I started listening to audiobooks while drawing and writing intuitively. As I listened, I would often pause and rewind the book to capture small snippets that caught my attention. I would write these fragments down and respond to them with my own thoughts, questions, or drawings. Sometimes the response was analytical, sometimes purely visual, but the act of stopping and reacting forced me to sit with the idea a little longer.
Instead of letting ideas pass by as part of a continuous stream, I began to treat them as something that required time to process. The act of fragmenting information – breaking it apart and reassembling it through writing and drawing – became a way of moving from consumption toward digestion.
This approach resonates with the work of Priscila Fernandes, particularly her exhibition News Stand. In this project, Fernandes resists a passive relationship to current events by turning to drawing as a way of processing violence and terror. The title refers to the familiar newspaper stand, a site typically associated with the rapid distribution and consumption of information, while subtly disrupting that expectation. Instead of facilitating the quick intake of news, her work questions the speed and immediacy with which it is normally consumed, functioning as a kind of mental exercise in digesting what is difficult to take in.
Although Fernandes does not rely on a single method when creating the drawings, each piece operates as a deliberate act of archiving, documenting, and preserving moments of crisis and despair. Rather than allowing these events to pass by as part of an endless stream of information, the drawings slow the process down. In this sense, both Fernandes’ work and my own process interrupt the cycle of rapid consumption, replacing it with a more deliberate and reflective form of engagement.

Chapter 2: Metabolising

While breaking information into fragments serves as a meaningful step toward being nourished by it, it is only a step in a larger process. Fragmentation alone does not guarantee deep understanding; it simply creates a good condition for it. Just as breaking food down into smaller pieces does not nourish the body until those pieces are metabolised, fragments of information must be processed and absorbed before they become meaningful knowledge.
Philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche frequently used digestion as a metaphor for thinking. In his book Ecce Homo, he noted that a strong mind does not simply accumulate ideas but slowly processes them, absorbing what is useful and rejecting what is not. Knowledge, in this sense, must be digested rather than merely consumed. The thinker’s task is not to gather the most information, but to metabolise it and transform it into something that can actually be lived with.
This idea of intellectual metabolism became central to my own process. After fragmenting the information I was taking in, making it bite-sized in a way, I began spending more time sitting with these fragments. At first, I didn’t know what to do with them, but as I allowed myself time to reflect on them, I felt increasingly connected to them. It went against my nature to spend such an amount of time reflecting on them, but this part of the process allowed for a sort of metabolic function. I would dissect the intuitive drawings and notes, isolating the elements I resonated with and come back to them in a different context.
This slower form of engagement requires patience, something that feels increasingly incompatible with contemporary media environments. These days, value seems to be universally measured by speed: how quickly something can be read, watched, understood, or shared. Information is expected to move continuously, leaving little space for reflection.
Several artistic practices challenge this acceleration by deliberately slowing down the conditions of attention. One example is Marina Abramović’s exercise Counting the Rice, which I was lucky to see at Museum Voorlinden earlier this year. Participants are asked to sit for an hour, separating grains of rice and lentils, counting them one by one. The task is intentionally repetitive and slow, often provoking frustration or boredom. Yet over time, the act should shift the participant’s awareness and attention should become sharper, more deliberate. The exercise functions as a kind of training in patience, an invitation to remain with a task long enough for something deeper to emerge.
In a similar way, metabolising information requires resisting the impulse to move on too quickly. It asks for a willingness to sit with ideas even when they feel dense or unresolved. Just as the body needs time to absorb nutrients from food, the mind needs time to transform fragments of information into understanding. Metabolism, then, is not simply about processing information efficiently, but allowing ideas to take root.

Chapter 3: Discarding the Waste

The body’s digestive system involves breaking down and metabolising what is taken in, but it also involves elimination. Not everything that enters the body becomes nourishment. Some material must be discarded in order for the system to remain healthy. In my process, I’ve found that the same logic can be applied to the mind’s intake of information. Contemporary media environments rarely encourage this final stage of digestion. Instead, they reward accumulation. Feeds are designed to deliver an endless flow of content, inviting users to take in more and more information without stopping to consider what should be kept and what should be let go.
Designer and writer Silvio Lorusso critiques this culture of constant productivity in his book What Design Can't Do. Lorusso challenges the assumption that design must always make systems more efficient and frictionless. Rather than smoothing every interaction, he suggests that design can also introduce resistance and refusal. These moments of friction create space for reflection, disrupting the expectation that everything should happen quickly and seamlessly. [QUOTE LORUSSO]
This idea appears again in Lorusso’s project Shouldn't You Be Working?, in which he creates a physical manifestation of a browser extension that interrupts online browsing with messages that question the user’s productivity. Instead of helping users work faster, the extension introduces small disturbances that break the rhythm of constant activity, and by taking the messages out of their digital context and positioning them in the real world, Lorusso questions society’s relationship to productivity on a larger scale. By extracting these seemingly innocent messages, the work exposes how deeply the expectation of efficiency shapes our daily life.
Lorusso’s method of extraction and repositioning within a different environment resonates strongly with the development of my own project. Using the fragments I had isolated from my drawings and notes, I began creating text-based works that communicate vague yet thought-provoking messages in the form of diet-style labels. By borrowing the visual language of food packaging, the works frame information as something that can be measured, processed, and consumed. In doing so, they highlight the parallels between diet culture and information culture, questioning how both systems reduce nourishment to a set of optimized units designed for quick intake rather than meaningful digestion.
Other artists approach this problem of frictionless consumption by exaggerating the speed of information itself. In Sebastian Schmieg’s project Speed Reading, text is presented at a pace far beyond human comprehension. Words flash across the screen so quickly that viewers technically “read” them without being able to process their meaning. The work exposes the illusion of efficiency: exposure to information is mistaken for understanding.
Lorusso’s interruptions and Schmieg’s acceleration are different approaches to the same problem. When information moves too quickly, without space for reflection, digestion becomes impossible. Instead of nourishing the mind, the stream of content simply passes through it.
In my own work, this realization raises an important question: if so much of contemporary information cannot be properly digested, how should one know what should be kept, and what should be discarded? Learning to eliminate unnecessary input may be just as important as learning to process what remains. Just as the body maintains its health by discarding what it cannot use, the mind might require a similar act of refusal, a willingness to let certain information pass by rather than absorbing it endlessly. Discarding, in this sense, becomes an act of care.

Conclusion

This project began with a simple observation: I was consuming large amounts of information without processing it – feeling nourished by it. The more efficiently information was delivered to me, the less it seemed to stay with me. What initially appeared to be a problem of attention gradually revealed itself as something deeper; a cultural system that encourages consumption but rarely allows time and space for digestion.
By approaching the mind as a digestive system, I began to think of information in stages: breaking it down, metabolising it, and discarding what cannot be used. This metaphor helped frame both my research and my design practice. Fragmenting information through drawing and writing slowed down the process of intake, creating small moments where ideas could be examined rather than immediately replaced by new ones.
Taken together, these references suggest that the problem is not a lack of information, but the conditions under which it is delivered. When communication systems are designed primarily for efficiency, ideas become scraps that pass through us rather than nutrients that sustain us.
Graphic design plays an active role in shaping these conditions, influencing how information is taken in, how quickly it moves, and whether there is space for reflection. Because of this, design does not only communicate information; it also determines how that information can be digested. Rather than optimizing communication for faster consumption, design might also be used to introduce moments of friction: pauses, interruptions, and structures that resist immediate comprehension. These moments slow the flow of information just enough to allow for digestion.
If contemporary culture encourages us to consume endlessly, perhaps design can help us learn how to metabolise – and sometimes refuse – what we take in. The goal, then, is not to consume more information, but to become more selective about what we allow to nourish us. In a culture that constantly asks us to keep scrolling, perhaps the most radical gesture is simply to stop and digest what we have already taken in.
